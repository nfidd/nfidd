Nowcasting: the right-truncation problem

Counts for the most recent dates are incomplete — events are reported only after a delay, so today's column always looks artificially low. Nowcasting estimates what those recent counts will become once every late report has arrived, using the reporting triangle and the delay distribution.

Why the present looks like it's falling

A delay in reporting masquerades as a decline

Epidemiological data is never available the instant an event happens. Someone develops symptoms, then days pass before that onset is tested, collated, cleaned and finally entered into the dataset an analyst sees. We call the moment a record arrives its reporting time. Because of this lag, when we look at the data now we have only seen some of the events that have actually occurred recently — the rest are still in the post.

The damage is concentrated at the right edge of the time series. A date two weeks ago has had time for nearly all its reports to land, so its count is essentially complete. A date from yesterday has had almost no time, so we see only a fraction of its eventual total. Plotted naively, this makes the curve dip toward the present — and that dip is easy to mistake for the epidemic turning over.

In the NFIDD session, onsets cut off at day 70 appear to be flattening and declining, while the final dataset shows they were still rising. The apparent downturn was an artefact of reporting delay — pure right truncation.

Two quantities are in play, and keeping them straight is the whole game:

  • N(t) — the eventual count for reference date t (when the event happened), once every report is in. This is what we want.
  • observed(t) — what we have seen so far, as of "now" T. For recent t this is much smaller than N(t).

Nowcasting is the act of estimating N(t) from observed(t) plus knowledge of the reporting-delay distribution.

The simplest nowcast, in one line

Divide by the fraction you expect to have seen

Let F be the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of the reporting delay: F(d) is the probability that a report arrives within d days of the event. For a reference date t observed at "now" T, the available time for reports is T − t, so the fraction we expect to have seen is:

p(t) = F(T − t)   // fraction of date t's reports in by now

If the eventual count is N(t), then on average we observe a thinned-down version of it, and a simple nowcast just inverts that thinning:

observed(t) ≈ N(t) · F(T − t)   →   nowcast(t) = observed(t)F(T − t)

This is the multiplicative or scaling nowcast. The delay here is a discretised lognormal (the NFIDD session uses plnorm with meanlog 1, sdlog 0.5). From a target mean m and spread s the lognormal parameters are recovered as σ² = ln(1 + s²/m²) and μ = ln m − σ²/2, giving F(x) = Φ((ln x − μ) / σ).

Why the uncertainty explodes at the edge. When F(T − t) is small — say we have seen 10% of a recent day's reports — we are multiplying a tiny, noisy observed count by a large number (here, ×10). A binomial-completion argument gives a band that scales like nowcast(t)·√((1−p)/p) per √(observed): as p → 0 this blows up, which is exactly the flared band in the figure above. Extrapolating from few reports is intrinsically uncertain.

This one-liner is genuinely useful as a baseline, but it embeds strong assumptions — that the delay distribution is known and stable, and that recent dates behave like older ones. The NFIDD course builds up from here to full Bayesian models (a simple model, then a geometric-random-walk model) that share statistical strength across dates and propagate uncertainty properly. The scaling nowcast is the intuition those models formalise.

The reporting triangle

Why it's a triangle, and why recent rows are nearly empty

Lay the data out as a grid. Each row is a reference date t (when the event happened); each column is a reporting delay d = 0, 1, 2, … days. Cell (t, d) holds the events from date t that were reported d days later. At "now" T we can only have observed cells with t + d ≤ T — a report can't arrive before the present. That constraint carves the grid into two pieces:

  • An upper-left triangle of observed cells (teal). Old dates at the top are fully filled; their row is complete.
  • A lower-right triangle of not-yet-reported cells (grey hatch). The most recent date, at the bottom, has only its delay-0 cell observed — almost the whole row is still in the future.

The row sum over the teal cells is observed(t); the full row sum (teal + grey) is N(t). Nowcasting is the job of filling in the grey triangle. Press play to advance "now" and watch the triangle fill diagonally as time passes — every report that lands moves one grey cell to teal.

Cell shading depth reflects the expected number of reports at that delay (darker = more reports), so you can see the bulk of each row's mass cluster near small delays around the delay mean.

The name is literal: the boundary t + d = T is a diagonal, and "observed" is everything above it — a triangle. The reason recent dates are unreliable is geometric: their rows simply have fewer cells inside the triangle, so less of their total has been seen.

Things worth remembering

  • The dip is not a signal. A downturn in the last few days of a real-time curve is, by default, a reporting artefact until a nowcast says otherwise. Never call a peak off the right edge of raw data.
  • Reporting by date of report avoids truncation but answers a different question. Counting by when reports arrived isn't subject to revision, but it doesn't tell you how many people actually fell ill on a given day — and it inherits weekend and holiday reporting patterns.
  • Uncertainty must grow toward the present. Any honest nowcast widens at the right edge. A nowcast that looks confident about yesterday is hiding the fact that it has barely any data for yesterday.
  • Get the delay wrong and the nowcast is wrong. The scaling factor 1/F(T − t) is only as good as F. In practice the delay is estimated from data (and can drift over time), which is why the course separates "nowcasting with a known delay" from the harder real problem.
  • The reporting triangle is the data structure behind every method. Simple scaling, the renewal-based Bayesian models in the course, and dedicated tools like baselinenowcast all reduce to filling in that lower-right triangle.
The big picture. Right truncation makes the present look better (lower) than it is. Nowcasting reverses the thinning imposed by reporting delay — observed = N·F, so nowcast = observed / F — and the reporting triangle shows exactly which counts need that correction and how much data each one has to spare.
