What is a forecast?

A forecast extends the latent epidemic process into the future — beyond where the data ends. A good forecast is not a single line but a distribution, and its hallmark is uncertainty that grows with the horizon: the characteristic fan. This page builds that fan from a random walk and shows why the interval, not the central line, is the product.

Forecasting sits on a continuum

Past, present, future — the same latent process, asked about at different times

Estimating an epidemic in real time is really one question — what is the latent infection process doing? — asked at three points in time. The methods you meet in this course are three slices of that single continuum:

TaskAsks about…Relative to the data
R(t) estimationthe mechanism — how fast transmission is changingwithin the data
Nowcastingthe present — what today's count will become once delays resolveat the edge of the data
Forecastingthe future — what happens nextbeyond the data

In the session, the forecasting model is the renewal-equation model used for R(t) and nowcasting — simply extended into the future. You drop the likelihood past "now", let the random walk on R(t) keep wandering, and read off the simulated onsets. Nothing new is invented; the model is just run forward.

A forecast is a probabilistic statement about the future. It must come with uncertainty, and — because we are extrapolating beyond anything the data pins down — that uncertainty has to grow the further ahead we look. A forecast that stays equally confident at 1 day and 28 days ahead is almost certainly wrong about something.

The same paradigm as everywhere else in the course applies: maximise sharpness subject to calibration (Gneiting et al., 2007). Be as narrow as you can get away with, but never narrower than honesty allows.

A forecast in one equation: the random walk

Why the fan opens like the square root of the horizon

Epidemic incidence grows and shrinks multiplicatively, so we model the log of incidence. The simplest honest forecast is a random walk: each day, log-incidence takes the previous value plus a small drift and a random shock. Starting from the last observed value ynow (on the log scale), the value h steps ahead is a sum of h independent shocks — which is Normal:

log ynow+h ~ Normal( μh, σh )    with   μh = ynow + drift·h    σh = σ √h

Two ingredients drive everything:

  • The mean μh walks in a straight line. With drift = 0 (a pure random walk) the central forecast is flat — "tomorrow looks like today". With a non-zero drift it continues the recent slope.
  • The spread σh = σ√ h  grows like the square root of the horizon. Variances of independent shocks add, so after h days the variance is hσ² and the standard deviation is σ√h. This is the engine of the fan.

To turn this into prediction intervals we map quantiles back to the natural scale. The q-quantile of a Normal sits at mean + sd·zq where zq = normInv(q); exponentiating gives incidence:

medianh = exp( μh )     Qh(q) = exp( μh + σ√h · zq ) ,   zq = normInv(q)

A 90% interval uses q = 0.05 and 0.95; a 50% interval uses 0.25 and 0.75. Because the spread enters inside the exponential, the interval is symmetric in log space but skewed on the natural scale — the upper edge runs away faster than the lower edge falls. That asymmetry is exactly what makes epidemic fans look the way they do.

In short: random walk + sqrt-of-horizon spread + exponentiate is a complete, minimal probabilistic forecast. The next figure draws it.

Why the interval — not the line — is the product

Same central forecast, very different risks

A point forecast (the median line alone) can be dangerously incomplete. Here two forecasts share the exact same median trajectory but differ in volatility σ. They tell the same story about the "most likely" path — yet imply very different probabilities of crossing a decision threshold (say, a hospital-capacity line). The point forecast hides this entirely; only the interval reveals it.

Both forecasts agree on the median path (blue line). The shaded mass above the orange threshold is the probability of exceedance, computed from the log-Normal: P(yh > T) = 1 − Φ( (log T − μh) / (σ√h) ). Slide the threshold near the median and the two probabilities diverge sharply — the volatile forecast carries far more tail risk despite the identical "best guess".

The lesson: report the distribution, score the distribution. Two models with the same point forecast are not equally good — the one whose interval is honest about the risk is the one a decision-maker needs. This is exactly why the next session evaluates the whole predictive distribution (CRPS, WIS, PIT) rather than the central line.

Takeaways

  • A forecast extrapolates the latent process into the future — the same renewal model used for R(t) and nowcasting, run past the data.
  • It must be probabilistic: a single line is never enough. The deliverable is the prediction interval.
  • Modelling log-incidence as a random walk gives a Normal forecast with mean ynow + drift·h and spread σ√h — uncertainty grows like the square root of the horizon.
  • Exponentiating produces the fan: straight, symmetric edges on the log scale; exponential and asymmetric on the natural scale.
  • Drift = 0 gives a flat median that still fans out; non-zero drift continues the recent slope. Both are honest only if σ is honest.
  • The same median can imply very different risks. Decisions hinge on the interval, which is why we score the entire distribution, not the point.
