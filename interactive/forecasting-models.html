A spectrum of forecasting models

Forecasting models run from purely statistical (let the data speak) to mechanistic (encode how transmission works). The NFIDD course builds three of them on one renewal-equation core — they differ only in how the reproduction number R(t) is carried into the future. They agree near-term and diverge later, which is exactly why we ensemble.

One renewal core, three ways to look ahead

How much understanding of the mechanism do you bake in?

Every model on this page generates incidence the same way — feed a reproduction-number path R(t) through the renewal equation with a generation-interval delay. The only difference is the rule each model uses to extend R(t) past "now", the last day with data. That single choice places a model on the spectrum:

Random walk

Most statistical, simplest. Assume R stays around its last value. The median forecast continues the current growth rate roughly flat; uncertainty just fans out. A hard-to-beat baseline.

Differenced AR

More statistical. Continue the recent trend in R — its momentum. Can extrapolate an acceleration the random walk misses, but may overshoot when the trend won't last.

Susceptible depletion

More mechanistic. Set R(t) = R0·S(t)/N. As susceptibles run out, R bends down — so this model can anticipate the peak even while cases are still climbing.

None of these is "the right model". Each encodes a different belief about what happens next. The teaching point of the session: a combination of mechanistic and statistical thinking is powerful, but picking the single best model for a task is genuinely hard — so we often keep several and ensemble them.

⚠️ As the course warns: these toy archetypes are for building intuition, not real-world deployment.

The shared core: renewal with delays

Same machine, three different R(t) futures

The renewal equation says today's new infections are a reproduction number times a delay-weighted sum of recent infections, using the generation interval g(s) (how long after infection people pass it on):

It = Rt · Σs=1Smax g(s) · It−s

Up to "now" all three models share the same observed history (so it never reshuffles). From "now" forward, each plugs in its own R(t) rule:

  • Random walk: Rt = Rlast — frozen at the last estimate.
  • Differenced AR: log Rt − log Rt−1 = φ(log Rt−1 − log Rt−2) — keep the recent change in R, persisting it with damping φ.
  • Susceptible depletion: Rt = R0 · St−1/N — with S falling by each day's new infections.
Differencing makes the AR model non-stationary: instead of being pulled back to R = 1, it carries a trend. Set φ = 0 and the differenced AR becomes the random walk — the baseline is a special case of the more statistical model.

What you should see

  • When the history is still rising, the random walk just keeps it rising at today's rate, the AR model accelerates or decelerates with the recent momentum, and only the mechanistic model bends down and finds a peak.
  • Shrink the susceptible pool N and the mechanistic peak arrives earlier — depletion bites sooner. Make N huge and it nearly matches the random walk (susceptibles barely move).
  • Push φ toward 1 and the AR model commits harder to the current trend (more overshoot); drop φ to 0 and it collapses onto the random walk.

Uncertainty: agreement near-term, separation later

Each median carries a fan — overlapping at first, pulling apart with horizon

A median alone hides the real story. Here each model's forecast is drawn as a thin uncertainty fan (a central band that widens with horizon). Near "now" the three bands overlap almost completely — the models agree when the data still constrain them. Far out, the bands separate and barely touch: the models' assumptions, not the data, now drive them.

Bands show a central forecast interval. Overlap = the models can't be told apart yet; separation = the choice of model now matters a lot.

Why this motivates ensembles

No single model is reliably best — but together they bracket the truth

If one model were always best you would just use it. In the course's scoring (and in real forecast hubs) the ranking flips with the situation: around the peak the mechanistic model shines; once past it, all three are comparable; the statistical model occasionally over-grows early and occasionally wins late. The "best" model is a moving target you cannot know in advance.

Stylised forecast error of each model across phases of the outbreak. The winner changes — and the spread of the three (grey band) contains the truth even where any one model strays.

spread brackets truth: yes
Because the three sit at different points on the statistical↔mechanistic spectrum, their errors are not the same shape — where one is biased high, another is biased low. Averaging them cancels some of that error and hedges the model-choice gamble. That is the engine behind the next session.
