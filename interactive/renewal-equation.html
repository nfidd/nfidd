The renewal equation

How a single number — the time-varying reproduction number R(t) — drives an epidemic forward. The renewal equation turns today's transmissibility and the generation interval into tomorrow's cases. Above 1 it grows, below 1 it shrinks, and the epidemic peaks exactly where R(t) crosses 1. Everything here is live — drag the sliders and watch the outbreak respond.

The renewal equation

New infections are made by old infections — scaled by R(t)

Infections are not independent. Each new case was infected by someone who is already infectious. The renewal equation writes this down directly: the expected number of new infections on day t is the reproduction number times a weighted sum of recent infections, where the weights are the generation interval g(s) — the distribution of times between an infector being infected and them infecting someone else:

I(t) = R(t) · Σs=1∞ I(t − s) · g(s)

Read it piece by piece:

  • R(t) — the average number of secondary infections per infectious person at time t. It is allowed to change as susceptibility, behaviour, policy and seasonality change.
  • g(s) — the generation interval. It says how a person infected today spreads their onward infections across the coming days. We set g(0) = 0: nobody infects others on the very same day.
  • The sum is a convolution of the infection time series with itself — the renewal equation is the convolution from the previous session, but feeding back on the same series being generated.
The headline. Because g(s) sums to 1, on average each infectious person produces R(t) others. So R(t) > 1 → growth, R(t) < 1 → decline, and the epidemic peaks at the moment R(t) crosses 1. This is the single most useful fact about R(t), and the simulator below makes it visible.

This is more general than the basic reproduction number R0 (secondary infections in a fully susceptible population) and includes the simple SIR model as a special case — there R(t) = R0 S/N, falling as susceptibles are depleted.

The generation interval sets the clock timescale

A discretised lognormal kernel g(s), s = 1, 2, 3, …

The generation interval decides how fast a given R(t) plays out. A short interval means each generation of infections follows hard on the last, so the same R(t) produces faster growth; a long interval stretches the same R(t) out over more time. We use a lognormal distribution discretised onto whole days, with no same-day transmission (g(0) = 0):

σ² = ln(1 + ssd² / m²)   μ = ln(m) − σ²/2    g(s) ∝ F(s+1) − F(s)

where F is the lognormal CDF and the raw masses are normalised over s = 1…Smax so that Σ g(s) = 1. Drag the mean and standard deviation and watch the kernel — a typical respiratory pathogen has a mean of around 5 days.

Why the peak sits where R(t) = 1

When R(t) is above 1, every cohort of infections more than replaces itself, so incidence keeps climbing. When it dips below 1, each cohort under-replaces and incidence falls. The turning point — the peak — is exactly where R(t) passes through 1. There is a small lag set by the generation interval (incidence responds to R(t) with roughly one generation's delay), which is why the orange marker can sit a few days after the crossing rather than exactly on it.

R, growth rate and doubling time the link

The same R grows faster when the generation interval is shorter

A constant R(t) = R produces pure exponential growth, I(t) ∝ er t, once the kernel has settled. The per-day growth rate r and the reproduction number are tied together through the generation interval. We estimate r here by running the renewal equation at constant R until the day-to-day ratio of incidence stops changing, then reading r = ln(ratio). When growth is positive, the doubling time is ln(2)/r; when R < 1 the same formula gives the halving time.

Why the generation interval matters. R only counts how many people each case infects, not how fast. With a short generation interval those secondary infections arrive sooner, so the same R compounds more quickly: a higher growth rate r and a shorter doubling time. Slide the generation interval mean down and watch r climb even though R has not moved.

Takeaways

  • The renewal equation I(t) = R(t)·Σs I(t−s) g(s) generates incidence from the reproduction number and the generation interval — a convolution of the infection series with itself.
  • R(t) > 1 means growth, R(t) < 1 means decline, and the epidemic peaks where R(t) crosses 1 (give or take one generation's lag).
  • The generation interval sets the timescale: it links R to the exponential growth rate r and the doubling time. The same R grows faster with a shorter interval.
  • R(t) is rarely observed directly. In the course you instead estimate it: invert the renewal equation in Stan, place priors on R(t) (e.g. a geometric random walk for smoothness), and propagate uncertainty — see the session.
  • Because this version is deterministic, the equation gives the expectation of I(t); fitting to data adds observation noise (e.g. Poisson) on top.
